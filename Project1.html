Options Pricing

The Black-Scholes model is a mathematical model used to estimate the price of European-style stock options, assuming that the stock prices follow a lognormal distribution and that there are no arbitrage opportunities. The model takes into account factors such as the current stock price, the option's strike price, the time until expiration, the risk-free interest rate, and the stock's volatility. The model assumes that the option can be continuously hedged using a portfolio of the underlying stock and the risk-free asset, and provides a formula for determining the fair price of the option.

Numerical Methods

For some variations of the Black-Scholes model there are analytical solutions for theoretical pricing of options. Although very informative, most cases of options pricing cannot be solved analytically. In this project some of the most widely used numerical methods for solving the Black-Scholes equation was implemented. Using, for example, the Monte-Carlo method to simulate random price paths of arbitrary stocks (Figure 1) most options can be priced.

Figure 1: The simulation show Monte-Carlo-generated price paths for the same stock. Using all simulations, some average properties can be derived and used for options pricing.

Usecases, Limitations and Discussion

The project aims to compare different numerical solvers and evaluate when, where and why the different approaches are appropriate to use. Some limitations of the numerical methods are discussed together with possible workarounds and solutions. Some final remarks are also given about The Greeks used in the quantitative field of options pricing in the stock market.
